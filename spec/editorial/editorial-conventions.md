# Editorial Conventions

*Structural and formatting conventions for the book's content. For writing mechanics (punctuation, numbers, terminology, footnotes), see [style-guide.md](style-guide.md). For voice, audience, and tonal guidelines, see [voice-and-audience.md](voice-and-audience.md).*

---

- **Claude as default example:** This book uses Claude as its primary example throughout, the way a driving manual uses one car. Every strategy, technique, and spec pattern works identically with ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and any other capable AI assistant. Chapter 3 covers the rotation strategy for using multiple free tools without hitting limits. The skill being taught is AI literacy, not Claude proficiency. See the [style guide](style-guide.md) for the "your Agent" terminology convention.
- **Links:** Liberally. If a topic is required to understand, there should be a link. Link episode information to the relevant Wikipedia page (AKA Federation Database), link to instructions on using the tools, not just the AI Apps, but CommonMark, Anglesite, etc. Links are a form of citation, the only true currency in the Federation. To be cited is to be immortal. See the [style guide](style-guide.md) for citation link conventions.
- **Episode references:** Use the Reddit-style format `Show:SxEy "Episode Title"` linked to the episode's Wikipedia page. Examples: `[Seinfeld:S4E3 "The Pitch"](url)`, `[Friends:S5E16 "The One with the Cop"](url)`, `[Star Trek TNG:S6E12 "Ship in a Bottle"](url)`. For season-level references without a specific episode: `[Home Improvement:S1](url)`. For full-series references: `[Married with Children:S1–S11](url)`. The format is consistent, scannable, and gives the reader enough to find the episode. Year follows after a comma: `[Seinfeld:S2E11 "The Chinese Restaurant"](url), 1992`.
- **Sidebars:** Six types, written as Djot fenced divs (`::: science`, `::: tip`, `::: fairness`, `::: meme`, `::: spec`, `::: also`). `[SCIENCE]` ties best practices to peer-reviewed research. `[TIP]` gives a one-line spec technique. `[FAIRNESS]` flags structural unfairness, systemic inequality, or AI bias that affects the reader's situation — and what the reader should do about it. This includes racial and economic disparities in how systems operate (eviction, disaster impact, disability discrimination), not only AI-specific limitations. `[MEME]` uses a cultural touchstone to compress a concept — must earn its place; memes that merely signal cultural familiarity without doing conceptual work are cut. `[SPEC]` shows a full specification interview loop. `[ALSO]` serves two purposes: **(1)** translates a Claude-specific feature name into its equivalent in other major tools (e.g., Projects vs. Custom GPTs) — the original and still primary use; **(2)** surfaces supplementary resources, programs, or pathways that don't fit another callout type but represent a real information asymmetry the reader should know about (e.g., SCRA military protections, SHIP counseling, housing voucher programs, crisis hotlines). Short: one to three lines maximum. Tone: matter-of-fact, not a disclaimer. For tool-translation callouts: readers using Claude skip it; readers using other tools find their vocabulary. Target: at least one tool-translation [ALSO] callout per Field Guide domain chapter, placed in the Skill where the feature difference is most relevant to the reader's workflow.
- **Chapter epigraphs:** One per chapter. Should be from the Cultural Reference Sheet or directly relevant research. No corporate quotes. No motivational quotes. Simpsons, TNG, and Adams are canon. Include an art brief. Placement: the epigraph opens the chapter immediately after the chapter heading and subtitle, before the first horizontal rule. Strategy chapters use the TRINITRON pixel art block as the epigraph, followed by the caption, a horizontal rule, the episode prose scene ('The episode:' / 'The lesson:'), and a closing quote.
- **Adversarial citation of industry statements:** Public statements from AI industry leaders (CEOs, founders, researchers) may be cited in body text as corroborating evidence for the book's structural argument — never as endorsement or appeal to authority. The framing is adversarial: "even the people who profit from this describe it the way a consumer advocate would." The reader's default posture toward tech billionaires is skepticism; the technique leverages that skepticism by presenting the quote and letting the reader draw the conclusion. Requirements: **(1)** the quote must come from a primary, verifiable source (blog post, keynote transcript, published essay) with a URL in the endnote, **(2)** the surrounding text must explicitly disclaim trust in the speaker's motives — the reader is asked to evaluate the claim, not the person, **(3)** the quote must do structural work — it supports the class-analysis argument, not the speaker's reputation. Lead with the most politically general quote and build toward the most specific. This convention does not override "No corporate quotes" for chapter epigraphs, which remains in effect. See Chapter 1 ("What These Tools Actually Are") for the reference implementation.
- **Art briefs:** Every graphic is specified as a `.art.md` sidecar file that lives next to the chapter it belongs to. The filename (minus `.art.md`) is the image stem — the build looks for `src/images/{stem}.{format}`. The sidecar uses YAML frontmatter with three fields — `format` (file extension, typically `png`), `size` (full/half-left/half-right/margin), `alt` (accessibility description for screen readers) — plus an optional `rights` field that overrides the book-level license for a single image. The body of the file is the production brief for the illustrator. Alt text is required — no image ships without it. **Art direction is permanent.** The `.art.md` file is the authoritative record of what was asked for, the basis for revision if the image needs to be regenerated, and the source of truth for XMP metadata embedded in the image. It is never deleted. The chapter references the image with `[stem-name]{.art}` at the exact position where it should render, or `[_caption_]{.art stem="stem-name"}` when a caption is needed. The build pipeline embeds `alt` → `Iptc4xmpCore:AltTextAccessibility`, the brief body → `dc:description`, and `rights` (or the book-level default) → `dc:rights` / `xmpRights:UsageTerms` into the image every build, so edits to a sidecar show up in the PNG without a manual step. If the image does not exist, a placeholder box renders with the alt text and brief in an expandable `<details>` element. See the Illustration Spec for full syntax, rules, and examples.
- **Chapter opener art briefs:** Strategy chapters use the same `.art.md` sidecar format as inline graphics. The sidecar body contains the full Trinitron scene brief. The `alt` field provides the accessibility description. The episode caption is inlined in the art reference: `[_caption text_]{.art stem="stem-name"}`, which renders as a `<figcaption>` inside the `<figure>`.
- **"My Man Jeeves" Skill openers:** Each Skill in Part Two opens with `*My Man Jeeves:* _[text]_` — a one-paragraph opener in the voice of [Reginald Jeeves](reginald-jeeves.voice.md). The opener follows a three-beat pattern: **(1)** name the structural inequality or system dysfunction, **(2)** show how it creates the information asymmetry, **(3)** give the reader the tool that closes it. This pattern is what makes the voice work — it validates the reader's experience ("the system is broken"), establishes the mechanism ("here's why"), and pivots to agency ("here's what you do"). Keep all three beats. Drop any one and the opener either lectures, complains, or sells.
- **Skill anatomy:** Each Skill follows a consistent structure. Not every component is required for every Skill, but the order is fixed:

  ```
  #### [Prefix]-[#]: [Problem Title]
  *Strategy:* [combination of strategies]
  *See also:* [cross-references to related Skills]
  *My Man Jeeves:* _[Jeeves-voice opener]_
  *The Spec:*
  [monospace code block — the spec template]
  *What to do with the Output:* [concrete next action]
  *Common [Domain] Expert Role Specs:* [for Expert Role Skills only]
  [Callouts: SCIENCE, TIP, FAIRNESS, MEME, ALSO]
  <!-- RESEARCH NEEDED --> [editorial comments]
  [Footnotes]
  ```

  **Required:** Problem title, Strategy, My Man Jeeves, The Spec, at least one callout.
  **Expected for most Skills:** See also, What to do with the Output.
  **Conditional:** Expert Role Specs (only when the Skill uses the "Act as a..." pattern), Science Note (inline variant of [SCIENCE] for brief citations within prose).

- **Cross-references between Skills:** Skills that share concepts, strategies, or institutions should reference each other inline — e.g., "see also L-1: Negotiating a Contract" or "the appeal process in H-4 applies here." This is the DRY principle applied to a book: teach a concept once, reference it everywhere it recurs. The reader who arrives at Ho-5 (renter rights) from a lease dispute should find the contract-reading technique in L-1 rather than a second, lesser explanation of the same technique. Cross-references use the Skill ID format (`H-4`, `L-1`, `Ho-3`) so the reader learns the book's navigation system by using it.
- **Adult learning principles:** The Field Guide is designed for adult learners who arrive with a specific problem, not a desire to study. Each Skill respects four principles from [andragogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andragogy) (Knowles, 1984): **(1) Problem-centered, not subject-centered** — the Skill title is the problem, not the technique. "Appealing an Insurance Denial" not "How to Write Persuasive Letters." **(2) Experience is the resource** — the reader already knows their situation; the Skill teaches them how to articulate it to an Agent, not how to understand it. **(3) Immediate application** — every Skill ends with "What to do with the output," a concrete next action the reader can take tonight. **(4) Self-directed** — the spec is a template, not a script. The reader fills in their own details, reviews the Agent's output, and decides what to do with it. The book teaches a transferable practice (specification), not a set of answers.
- **Research-needed comments:** Two types of HTML comment mark editorial work remaining in the manuscript. Both are stripped from the ePub build.
  - `<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: [description] -->` — factual verification, citation needed, statistics to confirm, legal details to check against current law. These are standard editorial flags: the writer believes the claim is correct but needs a source, a date check, or a subject-matter expert to sign off.
  - `<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): [description] -->` — the emotional, psychological, or sociological dimension of a problem that deserves deeper treatment than a footnote. These flag where the Skill needs to go beyond "here's the information" to "here's what it feels like to be in this situation." The HUMAN CONDITION tag identifies the passages that make the book more than a spec cookbook — the structural loneliness of chronic illness, the shame after a scam, the parental guilt that research treats as a baseline rather than a variable. These notes often point toward additional [SCIENCE] or [FAIRNESS] callouts, expanded Majordomo openers, or entirely new Skills. They are the book's conscience.
- **The conservative-answer convention:** For high-stakes Skills — Health, Legal, Civic, Money, and anywhere the reader might act on the output without professional review — the spec should end with language requesting the most cautious interpretation. Common forms: `Give me the most conservative, legally accurate answer.` or `I will verify with [professional type].` Chapter 4 teaches this as a general principle. The convention ensures every high-stakes spec builds in the safety margin by default.
- **The veterans-benefits convention:** Where a distinct veterans pathway or benefit applies to a Skill's topic — and the benefit is materially different from what a civilian reader would receive — surface it in the Skill. Prefer natural integration (a sentence in the Jeeves opener, a named benefit inside a callout, a cross-reference in the Spec prompt) over a bolt-on sidebar. Do not force veteran content where none exists; the rule is *where appropriate*, not *everywhere*. When a benefit is named, provide the 2025-accurate program name, the statute or regulation governing it (e.g., 38 U.S.C. §§ 1521 for VA pension, USERRA for employment, SCRA for civil protections), and the application path or form numbers where known. Tie at minimum to: Health (VA appeals, Aid and Attendance, Vet Centers, burial benefits), Home (VA home loans, IRRRL), Work (USERRA, veterans preference, GI Bill retraining), Legal (VA disability claims, military divorce under USFSPA, SCRA), Money (GI Bill, SCRA interest caps), Life (Aid and Attendance for aging parents, National Cemetery burial rights), and Transportation (VA travel benefits, state veteran vehicle-tax exemptions). The convention is a completeness check: a reader whose service status would change the answer should never have to discover that elsewhere.
- **Reproducible assets:** Appendix A (Field Guide Quick Reference) and Appendix B (Spec Interview Starters) must be print-ready at 8.5x11 from the ePub export.
