# Editorial Conventions

- **Claude as default example:** This book uses Claude as its primary example throughout, the way a driving manual uses one car. Every strategy, technique, and spec pattern in this book works identically with ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and any other capable AI assistant. By default we will use "your Agent" rather than "Claude" and only mention Claude when writing about features specific to Claude, with asides or side-notes for the other platforms. Chapter 3 covers the rotation strategy for using multiple free tools without hitting limits. The skill being taught is AI literacy, not Claude proficiency.
- **Plain language standard:** Flesch-Kincaid Grade 8 target throughout. The reader is smart. Short sentences are not condescending; they are respectful of the reader's time.
- **Voice:** Dry, direct, structurally aware. "You" not "one." "Ask Claude" not "leverage the AI." Never "empower," "journey," "thrive," or "transform." The word "system" is used correctly — to mean the actual systems (insurance, legal, housing, medical) that the reader is navigating — not as vague motivational shorthand.
- **The structural argument is present but not belabored.** Each chapter can acknowledge once that the system is confusing by design. It does not need to repeat this. The reader already knows. Move to the tool.
- **No jargon without definition:** Every technical term gets a plain-language gloss on first use.
- **Footnotes:** Numbered per chapter, reset each chapter. Print = page-bottom. ePub = chapter-end.
- **Links:** Liberally. If a topic is required to understand, there should be a link. Link episode information to the relevant Wikipedia page (AKA Federation Database), link to instructions on using the tools, not just the AI Apps, but CommonMark, Anglesite, etc. Links are a form of citation, the only true currency in the Federation. To be cited is to be immortal.
- **Sidebars:** Six types. `[SCIENCE]` ties best practices to peer-reviewed research. `[TIP]` gives a one-line prompt technique. `[FAIRNESS]` flags moments where AI bias or limitations are relevant, and what the reader should do about it. `[MEME]` uses a cultural touchstone to compress a concept — must earn its place; memes that merely signal cultural familiarity without doing conceptual work are cut. `[SPEC]` shows a full specification interview loop. `[ALSO]` translates a Claude-specific feature name into its equivalent in other major tools — appears wherever the text references a named Claude feature that exists under a different name elsewhere. Short: one to three lines maximum. Tone: matter-of-fact, not a disclaimer. Readers using Claude skip it; readers using other tools find their vocabulary.
- **Prompt examples:** Formatted in monospace blocks. Always use realistic, specific language. Never abstract placeholders. Example situations should reflect the actual economic and social circumstances of the target reader — renters, not just homeowners; employees, not just entrepreneurs; people with high-deductible health plans, not just people with "insurance."
- **Em dashes:** Use typographic conventions throughout as specified in https://practicaltypography.com
- **Chapter epigraphs:** One per chapter. Should be from the Cultural Reference Sheet or directly relevant research. No corporate quotes. No motivational quotes. Simpsons, TNG, and Adams are canon. Include an art brief.
- **Inline art briefs:** Every inline graphic is specified in the markdown source as an `<!-- art ... -->` HTML comment block at the exact position where the image should appear. Each brief contains four fields: `file` (image filename), `size` (full/half-left/half-right/margin), `alt` (accessibility description for screen readers), and `brief` (production instruction for the illustrator or image generator). Alt text is required — no image ships without it. The brief is stripped from ePub output and exported to `dist/art-manifest.json` as the illustrator's work order. See the Illustration Spec for full syntax, rules, and examples.
- **Chapter opener art briefs:** Strategy chapters use `*[TRINITRON — 8-BIT PIXEL ART: ...]*` italic blocks for the Trinitron composition briefs. These serve the same dual purpose — art direction and accessibility — but use a different format because they are structural elements of the chapter, not inline images. The italic block is the brief; the caption below it is the alt text.
- **Adams-format Field Guide entries:** Each entry in Part Two opens with a one-paragraph mock Guide description of the situation in Adams' voice — wry, structurally honest, slightly cosmically despairing — before the practical content.
- **Cross-references between entries:** Field Guide entries that share concepts, strategies, or institutions should reference each other inline — e.g., "see also L-1: Negotiating a Contract" or "the appeal process in H-4 applies here." This is the DRY principle applied to a book: teach a concept once, reference it everywhere it recurs. The reader who arrives at Ho-5 (renter rights) from a lease dispute should find the contract-reading skill in L-1 rather than a second, lesser explanation of the same technique. Cross-references use the entry ID format (`H-4`, `L-1`, `Ho-3`) so the reader learns the book's navigation system by using it.
- **Adult learning principles:** The Field Guide is designed for adult learners who arrive with a specific problem, not a desire to study. Each entry respects four principles from [andragogy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andragogy) (Knowles, 1984): **(1) Problem-centered, not subject-centered** — the entry title is the problem, not the skill. "Appealing an Insurance Denial" not "How to Write Persuasive Letters." **(2) Experience is the resource** — the reader already knows their situation; the entry teaches them how to articulate it to an Agent, not how to understand it. **(3) Immediate application** — every entry ends with "What to do with the output," a concrete next action the reader can take tonight. **(4) Self-directed** — the spec is a template, not a script. The reader fills in their own details, reviews the Agent's output, and decides what to do with it. The book teaches a transferable skill (specification), not a set of answers.
- **Reproducible assets:** Appendix A (Field Guide Quick Reference) and Appendix B (Spec Interview Starters) must be print-ready at 8.5x11 from the ePub export.
