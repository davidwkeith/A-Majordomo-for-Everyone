# Cover Art Spec

---

*Materials — the complete inventory:*

- **#2 pencil (HB):** All drawing and sketching throughout the book — the house on the cover, the Trinitron on every chapter page, all structural and detail elements. Medium-dark, slightly waxy graphite on college-ruled paper. Shows pressure variation naturally. Smudgeable. Erasure ghosts acceptable and expected — the slightly wrong line visible beneath the right one. Not ink. Softer edges, more organic, more vulnerable. This is the material of thinking.
- **Multi-color ballpoint pen, 256 colors:** The pixel art on each chapter's TV screen is hand-colored in ballpoint pen approximating the actual 8-bit color palette — the NES/SNES/Game Boy 256-color set, matched as closely as available ballpoint colors allow. The colors will not be exact digital matches. They will be close. This is correct. Hand-coloring is always a translation. The slight wrongness of each color is part of the image.
- **Blue ballpoint pen:** Author name and CC licensing information on the cover, written by hand following the blue ruled lines of the notebook paper. Nearly the same color as the printed lines — the handwriting sits within the paper's own grammar rather than on top of it.
- **Red rubber stamp:** DON'T PROMPT. Two lines. Over the front lawn on the cover. See stamp specification below.
- **Helvetica Black (typeset, not drawn):** Title and subtitle only. Printed at the top of the cover. The system's font. See typography specification below.

---

*The notebook page — cover:*

Standard American college-ruled notebook paper. Blue horizontal lines, perfectly level. Red vertical margin line on the left. No paper edges visible — the paper extends beyond all four sides of the frame. The paper is the background.

**The cover paper is horizontal.** The lines run straight across, not diagonal. Unlike the chapter illustrations — each of which has the paper at a unique angle — the cover is level and still. The dynamism on the cover comes from the house being drawn at an angle to the page, and from the telephone pole cutting the margin. The paper itself does not move. This is the correct hierarchy: the cover establishes the system, the chapters play with it.

The red margin line runs vertically, precisely. The title's left edge aligns to it. The telephone pole is drawn in the margin space to its left.

*The paper angle system — chapter illustrations only:*

For the ten strategy chapters, the paper is at a unique angle per chapter — determined by the primary action direction in the pixel art on screen. Blue lines extend the compositional vectors of each image. No two chapters share the same skew. The cover angle (horizontal) is the baseline from which all chapter angles depart.

**Paper angle reference table:**
```
Cover:        horizontal — level, still. The system at rest.
Strategy 0:   George's expansive outward gesture — roughly horizontal, slight upward arc
Strategy 1:   Staircase rise — diagonal upper-right, ~30-35 degrees
Strategy 2:   Tim and Jill's forward lean toward doctor — ~10-15 degrees forward-down
Strategy 3:   Murphy's upward ceiling-gaze — ~20 degrees counter-clockwise
Strategy 4:   Kramer's lean over railing — steep downward plunge, ~40 degrees
Strategy 5:   Ross's gaze to phone — nearly level, ~5-8 degrees
Strategy 6:   Tim's drill angle — down-left, ~45 degrees
Strategy 7:   Roseanne and Dan's forward lean over table — ~15-20 degrees forward
Strategy 8:   Al's horizontal point — dead level or nearly so
Strategy 9:   Holodeck grid line direction — ~30 degrees, notebook lines = holodeck grid
```
Map all eleven simultaneously before finalizing any. No two angles the same.

*The composition — cover:*

The house is drawn from across the street, at a slight three-quarter angle — the sitcom establishing shot. Not straight-on. You are at the curb, slightly to one side, looking at the house. The curb runs across the lower portion of the composition at the angle the house's perspective dictates. The lawn rises from the curb. The house sits above and behind the lawn.

**The telephone pole:**

Drawn in the left margin — in the narrow space between the left frame edge and the red margin line. The margin is supposed to be empty. This is the first rule broken. The pole is drawn there because the margin is right there and this is what you do when the class is boring.

The pole is vertical, running from the bottom of the frame to near the top. Its shaft cuts slightly into the left corner of the house — the house's left edge extends past the margin line, and the pole overlaps it, partially obscuring the left corner of the house. The way a real telephone pole would, if you were standing at the curb.

Telephone wires extend from the top of the pole to the right, drooping in catenary curves across the upper portion of the composition. They run toward the roofline and off the right edge. Drawn in #2 pencil, same medium as the house.

The red margin line and the telephone pole share a vertical zone. They are not the same line — the pole is in the margin to the left of the red line, the line is the boundary of the writing area. But they are parallel and proximate: the paper's institutional grid and the neighborhood's physical infrastructure, both running vertically through the left side of the frame.

---

*The house:*

Two-story colonial or ranch-style. Slight three-quarter perspective from the curb. Front face dominant, one side face partially visible. Left corner partially obscured by the telephone pole.

- *Exterior:* Brick or vinyl siding, beige/cream/tan. Black or dark green shutters. White trim.
- *Garage:* Attached. Panel lines rendered individually in pencil.
- *Front porch:* Covered. Screen door. Porch light.
- *Windows:* Double-hung, multiple panes, each rendered. Window boxes.
- *Roof:* Shingles in hatching. Brick chimney.
- *Driveway:* In perspective. Minivan or station wagon in compulsive pencil detail. Basketball hoop, slightly crooked.
- *Mailbox:* Post-mounted near the curb, distinct from the telephone pole.
- *Garden hose:* Coiled near the garage.
- *Tree:* One large oak or maple, canopy partially over the roof.
- *Curb:* Drawn in pencil across the lower composition at the perspective angle. Establishes street level. Grounds the image in suburban geography.

*The lawn and figure:*

Too-perfect sitcom lawn. Perfectly edged.

A figure mowing — shorts, t-shirt, baseball cap, mid-stride, pushing the mower across the lawn. Not identifiable. Generic enough to be the reader. Behind: darker pencil-hatched mowed strips. Ahead: uncut. Mower in compulsive pencil detail. Face not visible under the cap.

*The DON'T PROMPT stamp:*

Over the front lawn: **DONT** / **PROMPT**. Two lines. Large. Over the most active part of the composition.

- *Red:* Stamp-pad red, slightly different from the printed margin line — more orange, more saturated.
- *Stamp quality:* Uneven ink, bleed halo at letter edges, slightly off horizontal. Placed by a hand, not a machine. Applied after the drawing was complete.
- *Letterform:* Blocky, slightly condensed institutional rubber stamp. RECEIVED. APPROVED. CONFIDENTIAL.

*Pencil quality:*

High schooler's #2 pencil rendering. Accurate where the drawer cared — shutters, brick pattern, mower mechanics, telephone pole crossbars. Slightly rushed elsewhere — the tree, the figure's obscured face. Erasure ghosts visible in the graphite. The quality of someone who has stared at these objects for a long time.

---

*Cover typography — three zones:*

**Zone 1 — Top: Helvetica Black, left-aligned to the red margin**

- *"A Majordomo for Everyone"* — Helvetica Black. Large. At the very top, above the roofline. **Left edge of the text aligns to the red margin line.** The title respects the paper's own grid.
- *"Managing Your Personal Staff of AI Agents"* — Helvetica Black, smaller. Directly beneath, same left alignment.
- The red margin line runs from the title block down through the telephone pole zone. Title left edge, margin line, telephone pole shaft: a vertical conversation running the full height of the cover.

**Zone 2 — Middle: Red rubber stamp**

DON'T PROMPT, over the lawn. The intervention zone. Between the cold Helvetica at the top and the handwriting at the bottom.

**Zone 3 — Bottom: Blue ballpoint, centered, on the ruled lines**

Two lines, centered on the page, written in blue ballpoint following the horizontal ruled lines:

	David W. Keith
	CC BY-SA 4.0

The author name and the license. Two lines. Proximity does the attribution. The name is not repeated. Written in the color of the paper's own lines. Centered — the only centered element on the cover. Everything else is left-aligned or stamped. The centering signals that this belongs to everyone.

*The three zones:* The system at the top in its own cold font, aligned to the paper's rules. The intervention in the middle, stamped in red. The human at the bottom, written in the color of the page itself, centered, given away.

*Legal note:*

Original artwork. The house is a composite generic — not based on any specific property, not reproducing any filmed location. The figure is generic. The telephone pole is a generic utility pole. No identifiable people, locations, or properties depicted.

---

*Production notes:*

- Notebook paper: warm off-white, not pure white.
- Cover lines are perfectly horizontal. This must be exact.
- Title left edge aligns precisely to the red margin line.
- Pencil graphite shows pressure variation and erasure ghosts.
- Ballpoint pixel art colors inside: match hand-colored originals as closely as possible. Slight color wrongness is intentional.
- Cover and chapters feel like they came from the same notebook.
