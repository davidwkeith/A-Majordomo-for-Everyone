# Master Episode Index

*One anchor episode per strategy chapter. Each episode is chosen for three qualities: the problem is universally recognizable, the iconic scene is visually distinctive enough to illustrate, and the AI solution is obvious in retrospect. Verify all episode details before final draft — titles and season numbers are approximate and should be confirmed.*

---

**Strategy 0: Specify**
*Seinfeld*, "The Pitch" (Season 4, 1992)
**The problem:** Jerry and George pitch a TV show to NBC. George's concept is "a show about nothing." When the NBC executive asks what the characters do in the show, George says "nothing." The spec is so empty it produces nothing — and then they're surprised when NBC can't develop it.
**The iconic scene:** George and Jerry at the NBC conference table. George gesturing expansively. The executive's expression of polite confusion. The spec is absent. The meeting is doomed.
**The irony:** If George had been asked five clarifying questions — setting, characters, tone, conflict, what the pilot episode is about — he would have had to produce an actual concept. He might have written a good show. Instead he wrote the worst show NBC had ever seen, which was then greenlit, produced, and became a disaster. Specification first.
**Pixel art scene brief:** George leaning forward at a conference table, hands animated, across from two NBC executives in suits. The whiteboard behind him is completely blank. Jerry sits beside him looking quietly horrified.
**Caption:** *Seinfeld, "The Pitch" (1992) — George has a concept. He cannot specify it. This is always how it starts.*

**Strategy 1: Decode**
*Friends*, "The One with the Cop" (Season 5, 1999)
**The problem:** Ross buys a new couch and attempts to move it up the stairs of his apartment building himself. He has calculated the geometry of the turn. His calculation is wrong. He has not asked anyone to verify the calculation. He proceeds with absolute confidence. The couch will not fit.
**The iconic scene:** Ross on the staircase landing, couch jammed diagonally against the wall, shouting "PIVOT!" at Rachel and Chandler. The couch is objectively not going to pivot. Ross has not decoded the geometry correctly and will not accept correction.
**The irony:** The instruction — the physical reality of the staircase — was always there to be read correctly. Ross decoded it wrong, refused to recalibrate, and destroyed the couch. A second opinion on the geometry at step one would have cost nothing.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Ross on the stairs below, pushing the couch upward. Rachel and Chandler above, pushing from the other end. The couch is clearly, geometrically, impossibly stuck. Ross's mouth is open mid-"PIVOT."
**Caption:** *Friends, "The One with the Cop" (1999) — The staircase was always that width. Ross just never checked.*

**Strategy 2: Prepare**
*Home Improvement*, "The Longest Day" (Season 4, approximate)
**The problem:** Tim takes Randy to the doctor for a routine checkup that turns into something more serious. Tim, who has spent the episode confident and in control, suddenly finds himself in a waiting room with no questions prepared, no understanding of what the tests mean, and no framework for the conversation he's about to have with the doctor.
**The iconic scene:** Tim in the hospital waiting room, sitting still — which Tim almost never does. Jill is there. Neither of them knows what to ask. The doctor comes out and speaks in clinical terms neither of them is prepared to parse.
**The irony:** Tim spent the episode fixing things that weren't broken. He did not prepare for the one conversation where preparation would have mattered. Every question he should have written down beforehand has to be asked reactively, under stress, in the wrong order.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Tim and Jill on a waiting room bench, both leaning forward, elbows on knees. A doctor stands before them holding a clipboard. Tim's hands are empty — no notes, no list. The look on both their faces is the specific expression of people who have questions they haven't formed yet.
**Caption:** *Home Improvement, "The Longest Day" (approx. 1994) — Tim fixed the garbage disposal this morning. He did not write down what to ask the doctor.*

**Strategy 3: Draft**
*Murphy Brown*, "Respect" (Season 1, 1988)
**The problem:** Murphy Brown, returning from rehab, fires her latest secretary within the first hour and begins the process that will consume most of the series: the search for a secretary she can actually work with. The problem, which the show slowly reveals, is not the secretaries. It is that Murphy cannot articulate what she needs. She knows what she doesn't want. She cannot spec what she does want.
**The iconic scene:** Murphy at her desk, dictating something — a letter, a memo, a broadcast script — stopping, starting over, stopping again. The secretary sits across from her, pen raised, waiting. Murphy cannot get past the first sentence. The blank page is the problem.
**The irony:** Murphy Brown is one of the most verbally capable characters in 90s television. She can interview a senator without notes. She cannot write a simple memo without a first draft to react to. Getting the first draft is the whole skill. She never asked anyone for one.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Murphy at her desk, head tilted back slightly, eyes on the ceiling. Across from her, a secretary in a chair, notepad on knee, pen poised, expression of professional patience. The desk between them is covered in crumpled paper. The typewriter (it's 1988) has a blank page in it.
**Caption:** *Murphy Brown, "Respect" (1988) — Murphy Brown could talk for six hours. She could not start the memo. These are different skills.*

**Strategy 4: Navigate**
*Seinfeld*, "The Junior Mint" (Season 4, 1993)
**The problem:** Kramer and Jerry end up watching a surgical procedure from a hospital observation gallery. Kramer, leaning over the railing, accidentally drops a Junior Mint into the patient's open body cavity. Rather than navigating the correct process — telling someone immediately — they say nothing and hope the mint will be absorbed. The patient almost dies. Then unexpectedly recovers. The navigation failure nearly becomes a fatality.
**The iconic scene:** Kramer and Jerry in the gallery, looking down in horror at the operating table. Kramer's hand over his mouth. The surgeon looking puzzled. A Junior Mint, somewhere it should not be.
**The irony:** The correct process was simple: tell the surgeon immediately. The mint is a problem with a known solution. Not navigating the correct process — not knowing, or not following, the steps — turned a minor incident into a medical crisis. The steps existed. Nobody followed them.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Jerry and Kramer side by side in an observation gallery above an operating room, both leaning over the railing with expressions of dawning horror. The surgeon below is looking up at them. The scene is viewed from slightly above and behind Jerry and Kramer so their faces are visible in profile.
**Caption:** *Seinfeld, "The Junior Mint" (1993) — The correct procedure existed. Step one was "tell someone." Nobody took step one.*

**Strategy 5: Decide**
*Friends*, "The One Where Ross and Rachel Take a Break" (Season 3, 1997)
**The problem:** Ross and Rachel agree — ambiguously — to "take a break." Neither confirms what this means. Ross interprets it as a breakup. Rachel does not. Ross acts on his interpretation immediately. Rachel discovers this the next morning. The resulting dispute runs for three seasons of television and is never fully resolved, because the original decision was never confirmed by both parties before execution.
**The iconic scene:** Ross in his apartment the next morning, realizing Rachel is about to find out what he did. The moment before she knows. His face showing the exact expression of a person who acted on an unconfirmed spec.
**The irony:** One clarifying question — "when you say 'a break,' do you mean we are broken up?" — asked before either party acted, would have resolved the entire arc. The decision had two possible meanings. Neither party verified which one they had agreed to.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Ross sitting on his couch, early morning, phone in hand, the expression of a person who has just realized that what he thought the decision meant and what she thought it meant are not the same thing. The apartment around him is ordinary. The disaster is entirely internal.
**Caption:** *Friends, "The One Where Ross and Rachel Take a Break" (1997) — The spec was ambiguous. He executed anyway. This is the entire problem.*

**Strategy 6: Diagnose**
*Home Improvement*, "Pilot" / general Tim Taylor methodology (Season 1, 1991)
**The problem:** Tim Taylor, host of the fictional home improvement show Tool Time, approaches every domestic repair with the same process: form a confident theory, skip the diagnostic step, apply maximum power. The theory is always wrong. The power makes it worse. Al identifies the actual problem correctly before Tim begins and is ignored.
**The iconic scene:** Tim in a garage or workshop, tool in hand, about to do something to an appliance or structure. Al standing beside him, arms folded, with the expression of a person who already knows how this ends. Tim proceeds.
**The irony:** Al's diagnosis is always correct. Tim's is always wrong. The diagnostic information is available before the first tool is picked up. Tim's methodology skips from "theory" directly to "intervention" without the step in between. This step is the entire chapter.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Tim in a workshop, holding a large power tool at an angle suggesting imminent structural danger. Al beside him, arms crossed, with the patient expression of a person watching a slow-motion event he cannot stop. The appliance or structure they are about to work on looks fine — for now.
**Caption:** *Home Improvement, Season 1 (1991) — Al knew what was wrong. Tim had a drill. These are not the same thing.*

**Strategy 7: Research**
*Roseanne*, "Toto, We're Not in Kansas Anymore" (approximate — the episode where the Conners make a major financial decision under pressure)
**The problem:** The Conner family faces a significant financial decision — a major purchase, a loan, a contract — under time pressure, without complete information, and without the research that would have revealed the correct choice was different from the obvious one. The decision is made. The consequences follow.
**The iconic scene:** Roseanne at the kitchen table with Dan, papers spread between them, trying to make a decision from incomplete information. The kitchen is the Conner family's decision-making room. The papers represent a system neither of them fully understands.
**The irony:** The information they needed existed. It was not expensive or difficult to obtain. They did not have it because nobody told them to look for it before committing, and the other party in the transaction was not going to offer it.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Roseanne and Dan at the kitchen table, papers and bills spread between them, both leaning over the table. Their expressions are the specific exhausted focus of people trying to make a good decision from bad information. Coffee cups. A pen. A contract or form neither of them quite understands.
**Caption:** *Roseanne (approx. 1993) — The information existed. Nobody told them to ask for it before they signed.*

**Strategy 8: Assert**
*Married with Children*, "A Shoe Room with a View" / general Al Bundy methodology (Season 1-11)
**The problem:** Al Bundy, shoe salesman, knows that the systems around him are not designed in his favor. He is correct. He is almost entirely unable to do anything about it, because knowing you have rights and knowing how to exercise them are different skills. Al asserts his rights constantly. He almost never specifies them correctly, documents them adequately, or follows the correct process to enforce them.
**The iconic scene:** Al at the shoe store, one hand on the shoe he is trying to sell, the other gesturing at a customer or the ceiling in a posture of a man making a point that will not land. The NO MA'AM banner is somewhere in the background.
**The irony:** Al is not wrong. He is almost always substantively correct about the unfairness he perceives. He fails to do anything about it because assertion without correct specification and documentation produces nothing. The right was real. The exercise of it was not.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Al behind the shoe store counter, pointing at nothing in particular, one shoe on the counter in front of him. A customer of indeterminate expression sits in the customer chair. The expression on Al's face is the specific look of a man who is right and knows it and is completely unable to leverage that rightness into an outcome.
**Caption:** *Married with Children (1987-1997) — Al was right. He just never figured out how to say it in a way that worked.*

**Strategy 9: Create**
*Star Trek: The Next Generation*, "Ship in a Bottle" (Season 6, 1993)
**The problem:** Moriarty — a holodeck character who achieved self-awareness — has spent four years inside a program believing he has escaped the holodeck into the real world. He has not. He is living in a simulation within a simulation. The story he believes he is in is not the story he is actually in. He did not author his own narrative. Someone else's spec determined his reality.
**The iconic scene:** Picard, Data, and Barclay in the holodeck, realizing — too late — that what they thought was reality was another layer of the simulation. The moment of authorial revelation: the story was never what they thought it was, because they were not in control of the spec.
**The irony:** Moriarty's tragedy is not that he is fictional. It is that he believed he was the protagonist of a story he did not write. The chapter is about being the author, not the character — knowing which plot points are fixed, which are dynamic, and who is holding the pen.
**Pixel art scene brief:** Picard and Data standing in what appears to be a normal room, both with the specific expression of people who have just realized the room is not real. Through a window or door behind them, the grid lines of a holodeck become faintly visible. One of them is holding something — a book, a PADD — that should not exist outside the simulation.
**Caption:** *Star Trek: The Next Generation, "Ship in a Bottle" (1993) — Moriarty thought he'd escaped the story. He was still inside someone else's spec.*
