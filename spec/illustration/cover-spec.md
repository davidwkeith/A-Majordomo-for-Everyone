# Cover Art Brief

This brief extends the Illustration Instructions. The shared materials, style, and notebook paper conventions defined there apply here. This document covers only what is specific to the cover.

---

## Notebook Page — Cover

The cover paper is **horizontal**. Blue lines run straight across, not diagonal. Unlike the chapter illustrations — each of which has the paper at a unique angle — the cover is level and still. The dynamism comes from the house being drawn at an angle to the page and from the telephone pole cutting the margin. The paper itself does not move. The cover establishes the system; the chapters play with it.

The red margin line runs vertically, precisely. The title's left edge aligns to it. The telephone pole is drawn in the margin space to its left.

Paper color: unbleached recycled — a dull grayish-tan, not white, not cream.

---

## Composition

The house is drawn from across the street, at a slight three-quarter angle — the sitcom establishing shot. Not straight-on. You are at the curb, slightly to one side, looking at the house. The curb runs across the lower portion of the composition at the angle the house's perspective dictates. The lawn rises from the curb. The house sits above and behind the lawn.

### The Telephone Pole

Drawn in the left margin — in the narrow space between the left frame edge and the red margin line. The margin is supposed to be empty. This is the first rule broken. The pole is drawn there because the margin is right there and this is what you do when the class is boring.

The pole is vertical, running from the bottom of the frame to near the top. Its shaft cuts slightly into the left corner of the house — the house's left edge extends past the margin line, and the pole overlaps it, partially obscuring the left corner of the house. The way a real telephone pole would, if you were standing at the curb.

On the pole are a lost dog and yard sale poster. Dot-matrix printer paper stapled to the tree. Made with and early version PrintShop on a dot matrix printer. The details are unreadable.

Telephone wires extend from the top of the pole to the right, drooping in catenary curves across the upper portion of the composition. They run toward the roofline and off the right edge. Drawn in #2 pencil, same medium as the house.

The red margin line and the telephone pole share a vertical zone. They are not the same line — the pole is in the margin to the left of the red line, the line is the boundary of the writing area. But they are parallel and proximate: the paper's institutional grid and the neighborhood's physical infrastructure, both running vertically through the left side of the frame.

### The House

Two-story colonial or ranch-style. Slight three-quarter perspective from the curb. Front face dominant, one side face partially visible. Left corner partially obscured by the telephone pole.

- *Exterior:* Vinyl siding, beige. Dark green shutters. White trim.
- *Garage:* Attached. Panel lines rendered individually in pencil.
- *Front porch:* Covered. Screen door. Porch light.
- *Windows:* Double-hung, multiple panes, each rendered. Window boxes.
- *Roof:* Shingles in hatching. Brick chimney.
- *Driveway:* In perspective. Dodge Caravan in compulsive pencil detail. A single TMBG Flood sticker on the bumper. Basketball hoop, slightly crooked.
- *Mailbox:* Post-mounted near the curb, distinct from the telephone pole.
- *Garden hose:* Coiled near the garage.
- *Tree:* One large maple, canopy partially over the roof.
- *Curb:* Drawn in pencil across the lower composition at the perspective angle. Establishes street level. Grounds the image in suburban geography.

The house is a composite generic — not based on any specific property. The figure is generic. The telephone pole is a generic utility pole. No identifiable people, locations, or properties.

### The Lawn and Figure

Too-perfect sitcom lawn. Perfectly edged.

A figure mowing — shorts, t-shirt, baseball cap, mid-stride, pushing the mower across the lawn. Not identifiable. Generic enough to be the reader. Behind the mower: darker pencil-hatched mowed strips. Ahead: uncut. Mower in compulsive pencil detail. Face not visible under the cap.

### Pencil Quality

High schooler's #2 pencil rendering. Accurate where the drawer cared — shutters, brick pattern, mower mechanics, telephone pole crossbars. Slightly rushed elsewhere — the tree, the figure's obscured face. Erasure ghosts visible in the graphite. The quality of someone who has stared at these objects for a long time.

## Color — Same Pen, Same Palette

The cover uses ballpoint pen color from the master palette defined in the Illustration Instructions. Same pen, same colors, same pressure techniques. Unlike the chapter openers — where color is confined to the TV screen — the cover applies color directly to the pencil drawing.

The pencil does the structure. The ballpoint fills it in. Not everything is colored — some elements stay pure graphite. The kid colored the parts that mattered to them and left the rest.

**What gets color:**

- *Shutters:* Dark Green or Black
- *Brick/siding:* Skin Light or Skin Mid, light pressure — warm, not saturated
- *Roof shingles:* Brown, hatched over the pencil hatching
- *Tree canopy:* Green and Dark Green, layered
- *Lawn:* Green, light pressure — the too-perfect sitcom green
- *Mowed strips:* Green, heavier pressure behind the mower; lighter ahead
- *Vehicle:* One color from the palette, drawer's choice — the kid picked a color
- *Figure's cap and shirt:* One or two palette colors — enough to read, not a portrait
- *Chimney:* Red or Brown, brick-appropriate
- *Sky:* No color. Paper showing through. The sky is the paper.

**What stays pencil only:**

- Telephone pole and wires
- Window panes and trim
- Garage door panel lines
- Mower mechanical detail
- Curb and driveway
- Garden hose
- Mailbox

The color is casual, not comprehensive. The same kid who obsessively rendered the mower's engine housing did not bother coloring the driveway. The imbalance is the point.

## The DON'T PROMPT Stamp

Over the front lawn: **DONT** / **PROMPT**. Two lines. Large. Over the most active part on the bottom right of the composition. Askew from everything.

- *Red:* Stamp-pad red, slightly different from the printed margin line — more orange, more saturated.
- *Stamp quality:* Uneven ink, bleed halo at letter edges, slightly off horizontal. Placed by a hand, not a machine. Applied after the drawing was complete.
- *Letterform:* Blocky, slightly condensed institutional rubber stamp. The same family as RECEIVED. APPROVED. CONFIDENTIAL.

## Typography — Three Zones

### Zone 1 — Top: Helvetica Black

- *"Majordomo"* — Helvetica Black. Large. At the very top, above the roofline. **Left edge of the text aligns to the red margin line.** The title respects the paper's own grid.
- *"Billionaires have staff, now you do too."* — Helvetica Black, smaller. Directly beneath, same left alignment.
- The red margin line runs from the title block down through the telephone pole zone. Title left edge, margin line, telephone pole shaft: a vertical conversation running the full height of the cover.

Helvetica Black is typeset, not drawn. It is the system's font — cold, institutional, precise. It contrasts with everything else on the page.

### Zone 2 — Middle: Red Rubber Stamp

DON'T PROMPT, over the lawn. The intervention zone. Between the cold Helvetica at the top and the handwriting at the bottom.

### Zone 3 — Bottom: Blue Ballpoint

Two lines, centered on the page, written in blue ballpoint following the horizontal ruled lines:

    David W. Keith
    CC BY-SA 4.0

The author name and the license. Proximity does the attribution. Written in the color of the paper's own lines — nearly the same blue. Centered — the only centered element on the cover. Everything else is left-aligned or stamped. The centering signals that this belongs to everyone.

### Why Three Zones

The system at the top in its own cold font, aligned to the paper's rules. The intervention in the middle, stamped in red. The human at the bottom, written in the color of the page itself, centered, given away.

---

## Alignment Constraints

- Cover lines are perfectly horizontal. This must be exact.
- Title left edge aligns precisely to the red margin line.
- Cover and chapters must feel like they came from the same notebook.
- 8.5” x 11” Textbook Cover
