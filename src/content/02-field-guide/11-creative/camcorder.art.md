Hand-drawn #2 pencil sketch of a Sony CCD-TRV70E Handycam, circa
1995 --- the one every family owned or borrowed. Drawn with the same
obsessive technical detail as the Trinitron. This is the domestic
recording device of the era, rendered by someone who held one at
every birthday party and school play.

The camcorder is shown in three-quarter view, grip side facing the
viewer:

- **Body:** Compact, dark Charcoal plastic. The ergonomic grip with
  its textured rubber strip. The hand strap with velcro adjustment,
  slightly frayed.
- **Lens:** The built-in zoom lens with its small glass eye and the
  sliding lens cap partially open. "12x" printed near the lens
  barrel.
- **Viewfinder:** The flip-out LCD viewfinder extended, the screen
  showing a small 8-bit pixel-art rendering of the iconic Jaws
  shark fin breaking the waterline --- four to six colors from the
  master palette (Blue, Navy, Recycled, Black). The pixel art on the
  LCD follows the True pixel grid rules below.
- **Controls:** Every button rendered and labeled --- REC, PLAY,
  REW, FF, the zoom rocker on top, the red record button under the
  thumb position. The small secondary LCD that showed battery level
  and tape counter.
- **Sony logo** on the side, "Handycam" below it in the era's
  typeface.
- **Details:** The battery pack clipped to the back, the shoulder
  strap lug, the tripod mount on the bottom. A small Red LED near
  the lens that lit up during recording --- the light that told
  everyone in the room they were being filmed. The only color on
  the body of the camera; the LCD is the other color zone.

The camcorder is drawn as a single object, no hands holding it ---
a still life of the machine itself. The level of detail is slightly
unhinged, the way someone draws an object they have operated a
hundred times and could rebuild from memory.

**True pixel grid (LCD only) --- non-negotiable.** The Jaws shark on
the flip-out LCD is actual 8-bit pixel art, not a blocky
illustration. Render on a literal uniform grid of axis-aligned
square pixels. Every pixel the same size, every pixel a single flat
color from the master palette. Hard edges between colors, no
anti-aliasing, no smooth curves, no airbrush. Diagonals are
stair-stepped. The viewer should be able to count pixels along an
edge. The pencil drawing of the camera body does not have to obey
this rule --- only the LCD image does.

**No meta-elements --- non-negotiable.** The illustration contains
only the camcorder. No color swatches, palettes, legends, keys,
hex codes, callouts pointing at parts of the camera from outside the
object (the button labels printed on the camera itself are fine ---
they belong to the device), sidebar text, or any UI explaining the
colors or technique. No external "SONY HANDYCAM 1995" caption.

**Background: pure white, `#FFFFFF`, flat.** Not gray, not off-white,
not cream, not paper texture, not a notebook page, not a tabletop.
The camcorder is the only object; the field around it is pure
white. The build removes white to create transparency, so any gray
will show as a halo in the ePub.

**Watch out for:**

- NO hand or arm holding the camcorder --- still life only
- NO tripod under it
- NO modern DSLR or smartphone cues --- this is the specific
  early-90s shoulder/palmcorder shape
- NO graph paper or ruled-paper backdrop
