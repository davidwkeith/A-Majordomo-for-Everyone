#### C-1: Understanding a Ballot Measure in Plain Language
**Strategy:** Decode
**The Majordomo says:** *Ballot measure language is written by lawyers to survive legal challenge, not to inform voters. The official title often describes the opposite of what the measure does. The fiscal analysis is accurate but written for people who already understand the system it's analyzing. This is considered democratic participation.*
**The spec:**
```
I am trying to understand [Measure/Proposition name or number]
on my ballot. [Paste the official text or describe what you know about it.]
Please explain:
1. What this measure actually does, in plain language
2. Who supports it and why
3. Who opposes it and why
4. What happens if it passes vs. fails
Present both sides fairly -- I want to make up my own mind.
```

#### C-2: Writing a Public Comment for a City Council Meeting
**Strategy:** Draft
**The Majordomo says:** *Public comment periods exist by law. They are scheduled at times that maximize the difficulty of attending for people who work. They have time limits of two to three minutes. They are nonetheless one of the few remaining points of direct citizen input into local decisions that affect daily life. Showing up with a written comment is the difference between having said something and having been there.*
**The spec:**
```
My city council is voting on [issue] at their meeting on [date].
I want to speak during public comment.
My position is: [support/oppose] because [your main reasons].
I have [2/3] minutes.
Please write a public comment that states my position clearly,
gives my strongest reason, and asks the council for a specific action.
Write it to be spoken aloud -- conversational, not formal.
```

#### C-3: Knowing Your Rights During a Police Encounter
**Strategy:** Assert + Research
**See also:** C-7: Understanding Your Rights as a Citizen or Resident
**The Majordomo says:** *The gap between what a police officer may legally do and what officers routinely do during a stop is one of the most consequential information asymmetries in American civic life. Knowing your rights does not guarantee they will be respected. It does, however, create a record — verbal or otherwise — that matters afterward. The time to learn this is before you need it.*
**The spec:**
```
I want to understand my rights during a police encounter.
My situation: [traffic stop / stopped while walking / police
at my door / detained at a protest / witnessing an arrest /
general preparation]
My state: [state]
Please explain:
1. What I am legally required to do and say
2. What I am NOT required to do or say
3. What phrases I should use to clearly invoke my rights
   without escalating the situation
4. What I should document and how
5. What to do immediately after the encounter
Give me the most conservative, legally accurate answer.
```
**What to do with the output:** Memorize three phrases: *"Am I being detained or am I free to go?"* *"I am exercising my right to remain silent."* *"I do not consent to a search."* These are legally significant sentences. Practice saying them calmly. The calm is the point — rights exercised in anger are still legal, but de-escalation is a survival strategy, not a concession.

> **[FAIRNESS]** The experience of a police encounter varies dramatically by race, gender, neighborhood, and income. Your Agent should be direct about this reality. If it gives advice that assumes you are white and middle-class, say so: *"Adjust this for someone who is [your actual demographic] in [your actual neighborhood]."* The legal rights are the same. The practical risk is not.[^c3-1]

> **[TIP]** *"I was just [stopped / detained / searched]. Here is what happened: [describe]. Was any of this a violation of my rights, and what should I do now?"*

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Fourth and Fifth Amendment case law for lay audience. Terry v. Ohio (1968) for stop-and-frisk, Miranda v. Arizona (1966) for custodial interrogation. Critical nuance: "invoking the right to remain silent requires you to say so explicitly" per Salinas v. Texas (2013). The right to remain silent is universally known. The requirement to actively invoke it is not. This is the kind of information asymmetry that changes outcomes. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The fear response during a police encounter is a documented physiological event — elevated cortisol, reduced cognitive function, tunnel vision. Starcke & Brand (2012) on stress and decision-making: the conditions of a police encounter are precisely the conditions under which humans are worst at remembering and exercising abstract rights. This is why preparation matters — the knowledge has to be automatic, not recalled under duress. The body does not distinguish between a routine traffic stop and a threat. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): Racial disparities in policing are among the most extensively documented phenomena in American criminal justice. The Stanford Open Policing Project analyzed 200+ million traffic stops and found consistent racial disparities in stop rates, search rates, and outcomes. Pierson et al. (2020) in Nature Human Behaviour. The entry must name this reality without becoming a policy essay. The reader who is Black already knows. The reader who isn't may need the data. Both need the practical guidance. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Recording rights vary by state — "one-party consent" vs. "two-party consent." In most jurisdictions you have the right to record police in public spaces. The entry should clarify this and point to the ACLU's state-specific guides. Also: the practical mechanics of recording (phone placement, cloud backup, not reaching for your phone during a tense encounter). -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The aftermath of a police encounter — even a routine one that ends without incident — produces measurable stress responses that persist for hours to days. For communities with intergenerational experience of police violence, the stress is cumulative and heritable in the epigenetic sense (research on intergenerational trauma, Yehuda et al., 2016). The entry should acknowledge that "just cooperate and it'll be fine" is advice that ignores documented experience. -->

[^c3-1]: The Stanford Open Policing Project ([openpolicing.stanford.edu](https://openpolicing.stanford.edu)) provides the most comprehensive public dataset on racial disparities in police encounters.

#### C-4: Advocating for Change
**Strategy:** Draft + Navigate
**See also:** C-2: Writing a Public Comment, C-1: Understanding a Ballot Measure, W-5: Unions (collective action uses the same skills)
**The Majordomo says:** *The most common political advice in America is "vote." This is necessary and wildly insufficient. Between elections, decisions are made by the people who show up to meetings, write comments, call offices, organize neighbors, and apply sustained pressure to specific officials about specific issues. These people are not special. They are informed, persistent, and usually fewer than you think. In most local decisions, the margin between winning and losing is single-digit humans.*
**The spec:**
```
I want to advocate for [specific change] in my [city / county /
school district / state / workplace].
What I know about the issue: [describe]
What has been tried: [if anything — petitions, past votes, etc.]
Who makes the decision: [if you know — city council, school board,
specific official, or "I don't know"]
What I can contribute: [time per week, skills, connections, money]
Please help me:
1. Identify who actually has the power to make this decision
2. Map out what has worked for similar changes elsewhere
3. Draft a strategy — not just a letter, but an actual plan:
   who to contact, in what order, with what message
4. Help me write the first communication
```
**What to do with the output:** Start local. National change requires millions of people. Local change requires dozens, sometimes fewer. Your school board member has fewer constituents than your congressperson by a factor of a thousand. The math is in your favor at the local level.

> **[MEME]** [Alexander Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton) wrote his way into the Constitutional Convention. Debate and persuasion are not academic exercises — they are the tools of structural change. Your Agent can sharpen your argument the way a coach sharpens a pitch: *"Here is my position on [issue]. Argue against it as effectively as you can so I can strengthen my case."*

> **[TIP]** *"I have 2 minutes to speak at [meeting] about [issue]. I'm nervous. Help me write something clear and practice delivering it."*

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: The "who actually decides" problem. Most Americans cannot name their city council representative, school board members, or county commissioners (cite survey data if available). This is not apathy — it is a structural design feature. The entry should point to tools like vote.org, ballotpedia.org, and your Agent's ability to map decision-making authority for any jurisdiction. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): Political efficacy — the belief that your actions can influence government — is one of the strongest predictors of political participation (Bandura, 1997, "Self-Efficacy"). The decline in efficacy correlates with the rise of national media and the decline of local media. When your news is all national, every problem feels unsolvable. When your news is local, you can see the leverage points. The feeling of helplessness is a symptom of scale, not reality. Shrink the scale and the helplessness recedes. This insight is the entry's core argument and it is well-supported. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The "revolution or evolution?" question. Most successful social change in American history has been gradual, local, boring, and persistent — not dramatic, national, or violent. The civil rights movement succeeded through years of organizing, legal strategy, and coalition-building before the marches made the news. The narrative of dramatic revolution is more culturally compelling but less historically accurate as a theory of change. Marshall Ganz's organizing framework (Harvard, "Why David Sometimes Wins," 2009) is the best practical resource for civilians who want to do something. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Debate skills for civilians. The entry mentions Hamilton and Star Trek. The practical skill is argumentation structure: claim, evidence, warrant. Toulmin model (1958) is the standard but needs translation for non-academic readers. "Steelman the opposing argument" is one of the most powerful prompts in the book — it forces you to understand the strongest version of the position you oppose, which makes your own argument better and occasionally changes your mind. Both outcomes are useful. -->

#### C-5: Understanding Jury Duty
**Strategy:** Decode + Navigate
**The Majordomo says:** *Jury duty is the only context in American civic life where ordinary citizens have direct, binding power over an outcome that the government cannot override. A jury's acquittal cannot be appealed. This power is real, it is yours, and the system makes it feel like an inconvenience because an informed jury is harder to predict than an uninformed one.*
**The spec:**
```
I [received a jury duty summons / am currently serving on a jury /
want to understand the jury system].
My state: [state]
My situation: [general curiosity / first time / hardship concern /
currently serving — no case details]
Please explain:
1. What the process actually looks like from summons to verdict
2. What my rights and responsibilities are as a juror
3. How to evaluate evidence and testimony — what to weigh,
   what to question
4. What I should know that the orientation video doesn't cover
Do not give me legal advice about any specific case.
```
**What to do with the output:** If you are currently serving, do not share case details with your Agent or anyone else — that is a legal obligation, not a suggestion. Use your Agent for general education about the process, evidence evaluation, and your rights. After a verdict, ask: *"What just happened in that process, and what should I understand about it?"*

> **[SCIENCE]** Jury nullification — the jury's power to acquit even when the evidence supports conviction, because the jury disagrees with the law — is legal, documented, and almost never mentioned in jury instructions. Courts have consistently ruled that jurors have this power but that judges are not required to inform them of it.[^c5-1]

> **[TIP]** *"I just got a jury summons and I have [hardship: job won't pay, childcare, medical condition]. What are my actual options for deferral or exemption in [state]?"*

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Jury nullification legal history. Bushell's Case (1670), the Zenger trial (1735), Prohibition-era nullification. The entry should explain the concept factually without advocating for or against — just "this exists, and you should know it does." United States v. Dougherty (1972) is the key modern precedent. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The cognitive psychology of jury deliberation is rich territory. Confirmation bias, anchoring effects, narrative construction — juries are subject to every cognitive bias in the literature, and the adversarial system is structured around exploiting them. Pennington & Hastie (1992) "Story Model" of jury decision-making: jurors construct narratives and fit evidence into them, rather than weighing evidence independently. Understanding this about yourself makes you a better juror, not a worse one. The entry should help jurors notice their own narrative-building in real time. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The emotional toll of jury service, especially in violent crime or child abuse cases, is real and under-supported. Some jurisdictions offer post-trial counseling; most don't. Jurors are expected to absorb graphic evidence, deliberate rationally, and return to normal life without transition. PTSD symptoms in jurors are documented (Robertson et al., 2009) but support systems are minimal. The human cost of this civic duty is borne silently. The entry should mention that this is normal and point to resources. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: AI in the courtroom — predictive sentencing tools (COMPAS, etc.) have documented racial bias (ProPublica, 2016). Expert witnesses increasingly include data scientists. Deepfake evidence is an emerging evidentiary challenge. The tools entering the courtroom are the same tools the reader is learning to use in this book. Critical evaluation applies everywhere. The reader who serves on a jury after reading this book will encounter AI-generated evidence within the next five years. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Employer obligations during jury duty vary dramatically by state. Federal employees get full pay. Some states mandate employer pay during service; most don't. Daily juror compensation ranges from $5 to $50 — well below minimum wage. For hourly workers, jury duty is a financial crisis dressed as a civic privilege. The entry should cover this practically and point to hardship exemption procedures. -->

[^c5-1]: See *United States v. Dougherty,* 473 F.2d 1113 (D.C. Cir. 1972) for the court's explicit acknowledgment of jury nullification power.

#### C-6: Knowing Your Voter Rights
**Strategy:** Assert + Research
**See also:** C-1: Understanding a Ballot Measure (know what you're voting on), C-4: Advocating for Change
**The Majordomo says:** *Voter suppression in America does not usually look like someone blocking the door. It looks like a polling place that moved without notice, a registration purge that happened quietly, an ID requirement that costs money to satisfy, and a line that is three hours long in one neighborhood and fifteen minutes in another. Knowing your rights as a voter is defense against a system that makes participation harder for the people who would change it.*
**The spec:**
```
I want to make sure I can vote and that my vote counts.
My state: [state]
My situation: [first-time voter / recently moved / unsure if I'm
registered / had problems at the polls before / ex-felon /
want to vote by mail / need accessibility accommodations]
Please explain:
1. How to verify my registration and what to do if it's been purged
2. What ID I need and what to do if I don't have it
3. What provisional ballots are and when to insist on one
4. What to do if I'm turned away, challenged, or intimidated
5. The deadlines I cannot miss
```
**What to do with the output:** Check your registration now — not the week before the election. If your status is incorrect, you have more options to fix it before Election Day than on it. Your Agent can walk you through your state's specific process. Save the deadlines to your calendar.

> **[TIP]** *"I've never understood [ranked choice voting / jungle primaries / my state's electoral system]. Explain how it actually works and how my voting strategy should change compared to a standard primary."*

> **[FAIRNESS]** Voter ID laws and their effects are politically contested. The research is less contested: multiple studies document disparate impact by race, age, and income. Your Agent should present the law as it exists in your state, not as either party argues it should be.[^c6-1]

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Voter suppression evidence. Brennan Center for Justice has the most comprehensive reporting. Meredith & Morse (2014) on registration purges. Shelby County v. Holder (2013) gutted Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act; the Leadership Conference on Civil Rights (2019) documented 1,688 polling place closures in formerly covered jurisdictions following the decision. These are facts, not opinions, and the entry should present them as such. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The psychology of not voting. "Rational ignorance" (Downs, 1957) — the cost of becoming informed exceeds the expected benefit of a single vote — is the economist's explanation. The behavioral explanation is different: people don't vote when the barriers exceed the motivation, especially for people whose daily survival is already consuming their cognitive bandwidth. The entry should treat non-voting as a rational response to structural barriers, not as apathy or ignorance. The person working two jobs who can't get to the polls is not civically disengaged — they are civically obstructed. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): Voting as identity. Huddy et al. (2015) and the growing literature on political identity: partisan identity is now as central to self-concept as religious or ethnic identity for many Americans. This has implications for how people process voter-rights information — it is not experienced as neutral. The entry should present rights information without partisan framing while acknowledging that the reader may already experience it through a tribal lens. This is true of the reader and it is true of the Agent's training data. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Felon disenfranchisement varies wildly by state — Maine and Vermont allow voting from prison; most states restore rights after sentence completion; some require additional petitioning or gubernatorial pardon. The Sentencing Project has the most current state-by-state data. This affects an estimated 4.6 million Americans (Uggen et al., 2022). The entry should cover it practically without editorializing. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Voting system comparison — ranked choice voting, approval voting, jungle primaries, direct democracy. FairVote has accessible descriptions. The comparative evidence is young but growing — Alaska and Maine are the primary RCV data points. The entry should help the reader understand different systems, not advocate for any particular one. The reader who understands how their system works votes more effectively regardless of the system. -->

[^c6-1]: See the Brennan Center for Justice ([brennancenter.org](https://www.brennancenter.org)) and the bipartisan Election Assistance Commission for voter rights resources by state.

#### C-7: Understanding Your Rights Where You Live
**Strategy:** Research + Assert
**See also:** C-3: Knowing Your Rights During a Police Encounter, C-6: Knowing Your Voter Rights, L-1: Negotiating a Contract (for lease and employment agreements)
**The Majordomo says:** *Every human on Earth has rights. Some are inalienable — the [Universal Declaration of Human Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights) enumerates thirty of them. Some are constitutional. Some are statutory. Some depend on your state, your county, your city, your employer, and your landlord. The practical experience of those rights varies so dramatically by jurisdiction that two people with the same citizenship can live under meaningfully different legal regimes depending on which side of a state line they sleep on. The billionaire class knows this — it is why they choose their residence strategically. You should know it too.*
**The spec:**
```
I want to understand what rights and protections I actually have
where I live [or: where I'm considering moving].
My situation: [citizen / permanent resident / visa holder /
undocumented / unsure of my status]
My location: [city, state]
I'm concerned about [discrimination, housing rights, employment
protections, police conduct, healthcare access, education access,
family law, or "I just want to understand the landscape"]
Please:
1. Explain what federal rights apply to everyone regardless
   of status — and where the gaps are
2. Tell me what my state and local laws add or subtract
   from those protections (anti-discrimination, tenant rights,
   employment law, healthcare access, education)
3. Flag the biggest differences between where I live and
   national norms — what's better here, what's worse
4. Point me toward free or low-cost legal resources in my area
Give me the data, not the vibe. I want specifics I can act on.
```
**What to do with the output:** This is research, not legal advice. For any situation where enforcement of a right is at stake — discrimination, housing, employment, immigration — a legal aid organization or attorney should get involved. Many offer free consultations and pro bono representation for civil rights cases. Your Agent can help you find them: *"What legal aid organizations in [city/state] handle [type of case] and offer free consultations?"*

> **[TIP]** If you are considering a move: *"Compare the practical rights and protections for someone like me — [your situation] — living in [City A, State] versus [City B, State]. Include anti-discrimination laws, tenant protections, healthcare access, police accountability measures, and public school funding. I want the delta, not the Wikipedia summary."* This is more useful than a Reddit vibe-check and more specific than a "best cities" listicle.

> **[SCIENCE]** The [Universal Declaration of Human Rights](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights) (1948) establishes thirty rights that the global community agreed every human possesses. The American legal system implements some through constitutional law, some through federal statute, and some not at all. The distance between the UDHR and your daily experience is itself a measure of the information asymmetry this book exists to address.[^c7-1]

> **[FAIRNESS]** The experience of rights varies by race, gender, income, disability status, immigration status, and neighborhood — within the same city, under the same laws. If your Agent's answer describes the law without acknowledging the gap between law and enforcement, say so: *"That's what the law says. What's the documented experience for someone who is [your demographic] in [your area]?"* The data exists. Your Agent has read it.

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: State-by-state comparison of civil rights protections. The Equality Federation, Movement Advancement Project (MAP), and ACLU all maintain scorecards. The practical differences are enormous: employment anti-discrimination protections range from comprehensive (covering race, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, age, religion, national origin) in states like California and New York to near-minimum federal floor in states like Mississippi and Alabama. The entry should point to MAP's state equality maps as a starting resource. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Residency rights vs. citizenship rights. The practical gap: lawful permanent residents (green card holders) have most rights of citizens except voting in federal elections and some state/local elections, jury duty in most jurisdictions, and certain government employment. Visa holders have fewer protections. Undocumented residents still have constitutional protections (due process, equal protection, Fourth Amendment) but the practical exercise of those rights is constrained by fear of enforcement. The entry should enumerate these layers clearly. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The concept of "legal climate" — how safe people feel exercising their rights — is distinct from the rights themselves. A trans person in a state with anti-discrimination protections may still face hostile enforcement. A Black resident in a city with police reform laws may still experience disproportionate stops. The law on paper and the law in practice are two different systems that share a name. This gap is the central challenge of civil rights in America and the entry should name it without pretending the law alone resolves it. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED (HUMAN CONDITION): The decision to move — across a state line, to a different city — is often driven by rights and protections that people don't articulate as such. Parents of trans kids moving to more protective states. Women of reproductive age considering state abortion laws. Immigrants choosing between sanctuary cities and ICE-cooperative jurisdictions. These are rights-based migrations happening in real time, and they are not equally available to everyone. Mobility is itself a class privilege. The entry should serve both the reader who can move and the reader who cannot. -->

<!-- RESEARCH NEEDED: Legal aid access. LSC-funded legal aid serves approximately 1.8 million people annually but only reaches about 20% of eligible low-income Americans (Legal Services Corporation, 2023 Justice Gap Study). For every client served, another is turned away for lack of resources. Pro bono work from private attorneys fills some of the gap. The entry should point to: local legal aid societies, state bar association referral services, law school clinics, and the LSC website (lsc.gov) as starting points. -->

[^c7-1]: United Nations General Assembly. (1948). "Universal Declaration of Human Rights." The UDHR is not a treaty and is not legally binding, but its principles are reflected in the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (1966), which the U.S. ratified in 1992.
