The NCSA Mosaic web browser icon (1993), rendered in 8-bit pixel
art as it would appear at 32x32 on a Mac System 7 desktop.

**The icon shape:** A stylized blue ribbon curled into an "M"
silhouette --- two looping curves rising from lower-left and
lower-right and meeting in the middle. Each curl ends in a small
sphere bulb. At the center, where the curls cross, a small globe
of the Earth: blue oceans, tan/brown landmasses suggesting the
Americas, no political detail. The ribbon reads as 3D --- darker
shading on one side of each curl, lighter on the other --- but
within the constraints of an 8-bit icon: pixel-stepped highlight
and shadow, not smooth gradient.

**Palette (from the master palette):** Blue and Navy for the
ribbon (Navy on the shadow side, Blue on the lit side); Skin Mid
or Brown for the continents; Blue for the oceans; Recycled for
any highlight pixels; Black for the outline.

**Mac System 7 framing:** The icon sits on a small white square
the same width as the icon plus a one-pixel border, with a
two-pixel charcoal drop shadow offset down and right --- the
unmistakable System 7 desktop icon presentation. The square sits
alone on a pure white background. No filename label, no surrounding
desktop, no menu bar.

**True pixel grid --- non-negotiable.** This is actual 8-bit pixel
art at ~32x32 enlarged with nearest-neighbor scaling, not a 3D
render and not a blocky illustration. Every pixel the same size.
Hard edges, no anti-aliasing, no smooth curves --- the ribbon's
loops are stair-stepped pixel arcs, the globe is a tiny pixel
circle, the bulbs are clusters of pixels. The viewer should be
able to count individual pixels along any edge.

**No meta-elements.** No "Mosaic" wordmark inside the image, no
caption, no label, no version number, no NCSA logo, no color
swatches, no UI chrome beyond the System 7 icon tile itself.

This is what the internet looked like before it became five apps ---
a single icon on a desktop, promising everything.
