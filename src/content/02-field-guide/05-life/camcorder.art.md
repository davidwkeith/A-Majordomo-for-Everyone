Hand-drawn #2 pencil sketch of a Sony CCD TRV 70 E camcorder, circa 1995 — the one every family owned or borrowed. Drawn with the same obsessive technical detail as the Trinitron. This is the domestic recording device of the era, rendered by someone who held one at every birthday party and school play.

The camcorder is shown in three-quarter view, grip side facing the viewer:
- **Body:** Compact, dark charcoal gray plastic. The ergonomic grip with its textured rubber strip. The hand strap with velcro adjustment, slightly frayed.
- **Lens:** The built-in zoom lens with its small glass eye and the sliding lens cap partially open. "12x" printed near the lens barrel.
- **Viewfinder:** The LCD viewfinder flipped out, screen showing an 8bit pen drawing of the iconic Jaws shark.
- **Controls:** Every button rendered and labeled — REC, PLAY, REW, FF, the zoom rocker on top, the red record button under the thumb position. The small LCD that showed the battery level and tape counter.
- **Sony logo** on the side, "Handycam" below it in the era's typeface.
- **Details:** The battery pack clipped to the back, the shoulder strap lug, the tripod mount on the bottom. A small red LED near the lens that lit up during recording — the light that told everyone in the room they were being filmed. The only color outside the screen.

The camcorder is drawn as a single object, no hands holding it — a still life of the machine itself. The level of detail is slightly unhinged, the way someone draws an object they have operated a hundred times and could rebuild from memory. This is the camera that recorded every moment the family thought was worth keeping, operated by someone who never learned what any of the buttons besides REC actually did.
