## Core Framing

Assert is about exercising rights the other party is betting you don't know you have. This is distinct from Research (Strategy 7), which closes an information gap _before_ you commit. Assert is reactive — something has already happened to you, and you need to respond from a position of less power. The institution (gym, landlord, employer) already knows the law. They're counting on you not knowing it.

The unifying pattern across all three examples: the reader suspects they're being treated unfairly, and they're right — but suspecting unfairness and citing the statute that proves it are two different skills. The Agent's role is to name the specific right, cite the specific law, and lay out the specific process for exercising it.

## Example 1: Disputing a Gym Membership Charge

### Reader

Cancelled their gym membership three months ago. Called the front desk, told it was taken care of. Gym continued charging $49/month. Three charges totaling $147 since cancellation. Called again, put on hold, transferred, told they need to come in person with a written cancellation form. Feels like they're being run around. $49/month doesn't feel like enough to fight over. It is.

### Information gap

The reader doesn't know that:
- The Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA) gives them a formal dispute process through their credit card issuer, not the merchant
- A chargeback is a regulated process with specific timelines and protections, not "calling your bank to complain"
- Many states have automatic renewal laws (e.g., Illinois 815 ILCS 601) requiring specific cancellation procedures — if the gym didn't follow them, the charges may be void
- They can dispute all three charges, not just the most recent
- The credit card company is legally required to investigate within specific timeframes

### [SPEC] loop

- **Opening message:** "My gym keeps charging me after I cancelled. I called twice. They say I need to come in with a form. It's $49/month and I've been charged three times since I cancelled. What can I do?"
- **Clarifying questions:** How did you cancel (phone, email, in person), do you have any record, what state, credit or debit card, did you sign a contract with automatic renewal terms
- **Answers:** Called the front desk, no written confirmation, Illinois, credit card, signed a one-year contract that auto-renewed
- **Agent response:** Two paths — (1) dispute through credit card issuer under FCBA (formal chargeback), (2) assert rights under Illinois automatic renewal law (815 ILCS 601) requiring clear disclosure and simple cancellation mechanism. If the gym didn't provide a simple way to cancel, the auto-renewal may be unenforceable. Exact steps for the chargeback. What to document.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait — I can dispute charges from two months ago? I thought there was a deadline."
- **Agent:** Explains the 60-day FCBA window for billing errors, but unauthorized charges after cancellation have stronger basis. Credit card company will investigate regardless. Going forward: put cancellation in writing (email = timestamp), save confirmation.

### [TIP] callout

"Cite the statute." This is the Assert power move. "I'm disputing this charge" gets a form response. "I'm disputing this charge under the Fair Credit Billing Act, 15 U.S.C. § 1666" gets a different response. Your Agent knows the statute number. The other party already does too — they just weren't expecting you to.

## Example 2: Getting Your Security Deposit Back

### Reader

Moved out after two years. Left it clean — patched nail holes, scrubbed kitchen, had carpets cleaned. Got a letter 45 days later with a check for $380 of the $1,800 deposit. Deductions: $520 "cleaning," $400 "carpet replacement," $500 "paint and repair." No receipts. No photos. No itemization beyond three vague lines. Reader is angry but doesn't know whether this is legal or just how it works.

### Information gap

The reader doesn't know that:
- Most states have statutory deadlines for returning deposits (typically 14–30 days) — 45 days may already be a violation
- Most states require an itemized statement with receipts or estimates — three vague lines may not meet the legal standard
- "Normal wear and tear" is a legal term, not a judgment call — carpet worn after two years is wear, not damage
- Paint faded or scuffed after two years is wear — repainting between tenants is a landlord cost
- Many states impose penalties (double or triple deposit) on landlords who fail to comply
- A formal demand letter citing the statute is the first step; small claims court is designed for exactly this amount

### [SPEC] loop

- **Opening message:** "My landlord kept $1,420 of my $1,800 security deposit. The deductions are for cleaning, carpet, and paint but there are no receipts. I left the apartment clean. Is this legal?"
- **Clarifying questions:** State, how long they lived there, condition at move-in (photos or checklist), when moved out vs. when received letter, walkthrough with landlord
- **Answers:** Ohio, two years, no move-in photos (didn't know to take them), moved out March 1, received letter April 15 (45 days), no walkthrough offered
- **Agent response:** Ohio Revised Code §5321.16 — 30-day deadline, landlord is 15 days late. In Ohio, late return means tenant can recover full deposit plus damages. Itemization doesn't meet statutory requirements. Carpet and paint after two years = likely normal wear. Provides demand letter structure citing statute, deadline violation, and deduction deficiencies. Explains small claims court ($50 filing fee, no lawyer needed). Conservative-answer convention: verify with legal aid before sending.
- **Follow-up:** "I don't have move-in photos. Does that kill my case?"
- **Agent:** Burden of proof for deductions is on the landlord, not the tenant. Landlord needs to prove the damage existed and exceeded normal wear. No receipts and vague line items is the landlord's problem. Move-in photos would help, but their absence doesn't eliminate the claim — deadline violation alone may be sufficient in Ohio.

### [ALSO] callout

All four major tools can draft formal letters. Claude and ChatGPT generate well-structured demand letters when given the statute, the facts, and the tone. Gemini integrates with Google Docs for direct editing. Copilot works within Word. The letter is the easy part — knowing which statute to cite is where your Agent earns its keep.

## Example 3: Understanding Your Rights After Being Let Go

### Reader

Called into a meeting with their manager and an HR person they'd never met. Told "we're going in a different direction" and "this isn't performance-related." Handed a paper to sign — severance payment and release of claims. Walked to their desk to collect things and escorted out. Whole thing took 20 minutes. Now sitting at home, stunned, with no income, a paper they didn't sign, and no idea what just happened or what they're entitled to.

### Information gap

The widest gap in the chapter. The reader doesn't know:
- Whether they were fired or laid off — the distinction matters for unemployment, severance, and potential legal claims
- "At-will employment" doesn't mean the employer can do literally anything — retaliation, discrimination, and public policy violations are still illegal
- The severance agreement is negotiable, not take-it-or-leave-it — signing it waives the right to sue
- The release of claims has a 21-day consideration period (or 45 days for group layoffs) under the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act if the employee is over 40
- Their final paycheck has a state-mandated deadline
- They should file for unemployment immediately regardless of whether they think they were "fired"
- They should document everything they remember while it's fresh

### [SPEC] loop

- **Opening message:** "I just got let go from my job. They said 'we're going in a different direction' and it's not performance-related. They gave me a paper to sign about severance but I didn't sign it. I don't know what my rights are or what I should do first."
- **Clarifying questions:** State, how long they worked there, age (OWBPA threshold), only one let go or part of a group, any recent complaints (discrimination, safety, wage/hour), what the severance paper says
- **Answers:** Michigan, four years, 52 years old, only one let go, had complained to HR about unpaid overtime two months ago, paper offers two weeks' pay for signing a "general release of claims"
- **Agent response:** The most urgent and layered response in the chapter. ALL-CAPS headers:

  FIRST — WHAT TO DO THIS WEEK: Don't sign yet (OWBPA gives 21 days at age 52, plus 7-day revocation). File for unemployment today. Write down everything. Secure documentation — pay stubs, overtime complaint, emails, severance paper.

  THE OVERTIME COMPLAINT MATTERS: Complained about unpaid overtime two months ago, then let go. Michigan and federal FLSA prohibit retaliation for wage complaints. The timing is relevant. "Not performance-related" is the absence of an explanation, not an explanation. A lawyer would notice. Doesn't mean illegal retaliation — means the question is worth asking before signing away the right to ask it.

  THE SEVERANCE AGREEMENT: Two weeks for four years is low. Severance is offered in exchange for the release — employer is buying silence and right to sue. "General release" = giving up all claims including the overtime complaint. Can negotiate: more weeks, COBRA subsidy, neutral reference, removal of non-disparagement. Employer offered because they want you to sign. That means there's a negotiation.

  YOUR FINAL PAYCHECK: Michigan requires final wages by next regular payday. Check handbook re: accrued vacation (Michigan doesn't require payout by statute, but company policy may).

  WHAT TO VERIFY INDEPENDENTLY: Call Michigan employment attorney (free consultations common). File unemployment at michigan.gov/uia. Don't sign until you've spoken to an attorney or let the 21 days run.

  Conservative-answer convention: "Give me the most conservative, legally accurate answer. I will verify with an employment attorney before acting."

- **Follow-up:** "They said I have to sign within a week or the offer expires. Can they do that?"
- **Agent:** No. Over 40 = OWBPA requires minimum 21-day consideration period. Deadline shorter than 21 days makes the waiver unenforceable. If they said one week, they either don't know the law or hope you don't. You have 21 days. Take them.

### [SCIENCE] callout

Most American workers believe "at-will" means their employer can fire them for any reason with no consequences. The legal reality is narrower — at-will has exceptions for discrimination, retaliation, public policy violations, and implied contracts. The gap between what workers believe "at-will" means and what it actually means is one of the most consequential information asymmetries in American employment. Your Agent knows the exceptions. The employer's HR department does too.

### [MEME] callout (closing)

Al at the shoe store counter, making his point to no one in particular. Al was right about the overtime, right about the unfairness, right about the system. He never cited the statute. He never filed the complaint. He never asked what the law actually said. He just knew it was wrong and said so loudly, to the wrong audience, in the wrong format. Knowing your rights and exercising them are two different skills. This chapter is about the second one.

Al Bundy sold shoes for 11 seasons and suspected, correctly, that the systems around him were not designed in his favor. The difference between Al and the reader who just finished this chapter is not knowledge — Al knew. The difference is the citation. The landlord, the gym, and the employer all operated on the assumption that you didn't know what the law said. Now you do. The other party already knew. Now you're even.
